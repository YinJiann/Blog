Serving The Motherland: Singapore Edition

Last Updated: 24 Dec 2022

National Service: The bane of every Singaporean son.

Some are neutral about it, few love it and would go on to make it about their whole identity (you could say their lives peaked in NS), but I would say that majority of guys absolutely hated it.

I was no exception.

Back in school, national service was a concept that always seemed distant to me. Don't get me wrong, I knew it was something I had to face when I finished JC, but it just seemed always barely out of reach. Back then, the only exposure I had were the ridiculous stories that my brother would share with em. He made NS sound like something out of a fiction book, meaningless but yet unending in entertainment.

Back then, I confess I was completely sold by the propoganda of our government: the Ah Boys To Men films. Before we would even enlist, selected individuals would be notified that they are eligible for the prestigious elite units of the army, namely the Naval Diving Unit (NDU) and the Commandos. There were only two extremes to the reactions upon receiving the letters: utter despair and pure elation. I, of course, had hopes of receiving a letter from NDU, because I was thoroughly converted by the Ah Boys To Men: Frogmen film, but alas, the only letter I got was to report to BMTC.

In hindsight, this was a blessing in disguise.

BMT

I have forgotten a lot of things with time, so rather than writing in chronological order, I would instead write short excerpts of whatever comes to mind.

The Mono-intake Incident
I enlisted on the 4th of April 2019. I was one of the unfortunates of the JC batch to enlist in April. Having seen almost everyone around me enlist during January, I honestly felt left behind.

Before enlistment, my brother would tell me one and only one thing: Just don't get into mono-intake. Now for those unfamiliar with this term, mono intake happens when an individual wins the lottery ticket. Due to supposed manpower issues, randomly selected individuals would have a unit assigned to them prior to their enlistment, meaning that their future in NS has been decided on day one. This is unlike everyone else, whose units are often decided when they pass out from BMT (men)/ graduate from command school (sergeants/officers).

So why exactly is mono intake undesirable? Well it's because mono-intake only occurs for combat units such as Singapore Infantry Regiment (SIR), Singapore Armoured Regiment (SAR), Guards, NDU and the Commandos. These units are no fun. Once you are considered mono-intake, you can kiss your ass goodbye to all the good postings such as Transport, Navy, Air Force, etc.

But one should not lose hope if they are assigned into mono-intake. CMPB accounts for attrition rates in each intake, there will bound to be people who will end up being injured and becoming non-combat fit. As such, one actually has a slim chance of getting posted out. So for those wallowing in self-despair at the news of your mono-intake, keep your chin up king, you might just still have a shot at life for the next two years.

Anyway, I digress. When I boarded the bus to the ferry terminal, Matthew texted me a photo of the enlistment sheet for that day. He added on a message that I will not forget till this day.

"bro, I think you 3 SIR."

"Oh god"

I was in denial. I couldn't believe it.

How could I, among the hundreds of thousands enlisting on the 4th of April 2019, be so unlucky. I refused to believe what he told me and I placed blind faith in the hopes that this was just a massive elaborate prank. I only came to process reality when the lieutenant-colonel that greeted us in the opening speech started off by saying "welcome to 3 SIR."

I lost it.

There was nothing to look forward to anymore, my life for the next two years was doomed to be constant outfields, camo on, rifle cleaning and being one with the flora and fuana of our Motherland. In fact, I hated the idea so much I texted my brother that very day, asking him whether I should just sign on and escape this miserable life. To me, 5 years in a vocation of my choice was suddenly looking very appealing compared to 2 years of whatever that laid in store for me in 3 SIR.

My future self would thank me for not committing to this borderline insane idea.

Having such an underwhelming final meal also just made it worse.

I Almost Signed-on Incident
One thing I have to applaud the SAF for is just how efficient and insidious their recruitment tactics are. Having been thrown into a completely new environment that reeks of regimentation, everyone in the right mind would be looking for ways to escape this predicament.

And this is exactly where the SAF recruitment personnel come in.

In between the hellish training regime of BMT, recruits are dragged to lecture halls for recruitment talks, where carefully selected, charismatic individuals would try to sell you the idea of signing on. They would prepare over-the-top action films, pictures of overseas exercises and HUGE salary numbers to sell this too-good-to-be-true idea to you. On top of that, these recruitment talks are only spaced out over the course of two weeks for all three vocations (Army, Navy, Air Force). As such, you are inherently pressured to indicate your interest in a short period of time or forever miss out on this 'golden opportunity'. You don't even have time to consult your parents/loved ones about this decision over the weekend. I believe this eventually led to multiple impulsive decisions among the regulars.

I, for one, fell right into their trap.

Having heard multiple friends in the January batch decide to sign on as Naval Officers, plus the fact that I knew I was in mono-intake, the idea of being in the Navy was attractive. You get to live on a ship/submarine, enjoy the delicious food served by the Navy chefs, and go for overseas training exercises. I must also wholeheartedly admit that military weddings were extremely lit from the photos that were shown.

And just like that, I indicated interest in becoming a Naval Officer.

What followed were three more rounds of recruitment talks before the contract arrived in front of my lap. They gave you an ultimatum: you either sign it on the spot or if you choose to walk out of the room, you would lose that opportunity forever.

How predatory!

Thankfully, the recruitment officer was legally obliged to go through all the terms and conditions with us before we were allowed to sign, and there came a fineprint that even someone as dumb as me knew was a red flag. It went something like this: I will dedicate all my time to the SAF, I am obliged to report for duty whenever I am needed, regardless of circumstances (E.g. weekend/leave). Of course, this sounds perfectly understandable for an occupation involving the defence of the nation. How can one refuse to protect their nation in times of war because they applied for leave on that day.

But this clause really got me thinking, do I really value my time this little? Would I want to live a life at the whim of a single press of a button?

Absolutely not.

With that, I returned the contract, walked out of the lecture hall, and never looked back.

The pseudo-Toilet IC Incident
Ah, one cannot mention about army without talking about their toilets. They are surprisingly in decent conditions, obviously due to the insane standards of cleaning that happens in the SAF.

Every bunk had 16 individuals, and each person had a role. Roles ranged from Learnet IC, toilet IC, armskote men, and so on. I was the fireman of the section. Basically in times of a fire, I was in charge of grabbing the fire extinguisher and fighting the fire.

Since there was only one toilet IC, they would require the help of another person to clean the toilet each time. Now for some reason, perhaps I look very friendly, maybe I look like someone who finds it hard to say no, but I always ended up helping to clean the toilet. I dreaded every Stand-by-Area because the toilet IC would always approach me and ask me to clean the toilet. I hate it. I HATE IT SO MUCH.

Imagine plucking out public hair from the urinals, scrubbing the shower walls, cleaning the basin and washing the toilet floor. OH MY GOD, SO DISGUSTINGGGGG!

Maybe if I didn't look like a nerd, life could have been better haha.

The Buckethead Incident
Upon enlistment, everybody would be assigned a bucket, just a standard-sized red bucket meant for you to wash your clothes during BMT as you are only allowed to bring home a limited set of clothes each book out. By convention, these buckets were supposed to be kept faced down, at the foot of your bed. However, since you have to wash your clothes at any given opportunity, many would leave their buckets to dry on the clothing rack outside each bunk. It definitely felt wrong (because it goes against the army's idea of standardisation), but the PTP batch people did it and none of the sergeants ever gave us shit for it.

And so I followed their example.

Now one day, right as we were about the march off for lunch. Our sergeant said:

"There's something wrong, where do y'all think it is?"

There was confusion all around, we haven't done anything out of routine that day. There were mumblings among the platoon, so we ended up getting knocked down. After we recovered and we still did not figure out what was wrong, he then said

"Why the fuck are there buckets on the clothes rack. Who the fuck said you can do that. There are 1, 2, 3 ... 21 FUCKING BUCKETS!!!"

Oh shit. One of the buckets were mine.

Everyone knew we were screwed, we just didn't know how screwed.

What ensued was peak comedy. We were told to collect all the buckets that were on display and bring it to the first floor. Of course, an impossible timing was given so we ended up getting knocked down yet again. The culprits were then told to each grab their own buckets and fall in in file. Subsequently, we were instructed to "helmets on", and shout out the SAF core values, before repeating "I will not put my bucket on the clothes line again" twenty times.

If you were someone looking from afar, you would wonder just what the heck a 5 by 4 recruits were doing with their buckets covering their whole head.

Spending time inside the bucket was incredibly introspective, you start to wonder just what did you do in life to end up in such a humiliating yet hilarious situation.

The Choir Incident
The story above doesn't stop there. Right after the legendary buckethead incident, we were allowed to go for lunch. Upon returning to the coyline, our sergeants decided to have some fun with us, and this translated into a never-ending amount of Hentak Kaki commands. With each Hentak Kaki command, we had to end it by singing the "Count by the, count by the, left foot count ah" song. Now this was perfectly normal, just completely unnecessary and tiring. After what felt like 10 renditions of that song, the sergeant was clearly not satisfied. So he brought out a loudspeaker and said to the whole company.

"I've always had a dream to be the conductor of a choir, and I think today is the day I get to fulfill that dream. You guys see that old storeroom beside the vending machine? When I blast this horn, you have 30s to all fit inside and I want you guys to sing the Hentak Kaki song inside. You won't come out unless I'm satisfied."

Mind you, this storeroom was fucking old. It was completely empty, it was not ventilated and the air inside was so stale. What's worse? It had an area of about 4 normal-sized cars. How the fuck do you fit 200 people inside such a small space?

With the blast of the horn, everyone was trying to squeeze inside the room. Of course, we never met the timing so we ended up doing 60 push ups afterwards. Once inside, it was the living definition of claustrophobia. There was literally no space to move, there was, in fact, no space to breathe at all. I'm amazed at how those that were not naturally gifted in height were able to breathe in that situation. It soon became apparant that this was dangerous as people were claiming that they couldn't breathe. Anyway, we started screaming the Hentak Kaki song for literally our dear lives and we were eventually allowed out of that hellhole.

In hindsight, this was a prime example of calling the safety hotline and getting someone into DB.

The IPPT Incident
Ah, the Individual Physical Proficiency Test (IPPT), the bane of my existence in BMT. For the record, prior to national service, I was never that fit. I always managed to scrape by by scoring comfortably between the pass and silver bracket.

In BMT, IPPT is especially important if you are looking to go into command school. A minimum of silver is required, though I've seen a handful of exceptions who passed and still managed to go to command school. I, for one, wanted to go command school because I didn't want to go to 3 SIR straight away after BMT, so I worked my ass off trying to get silver.

Before I get into the story, you must understand just what was at stake here. It was the last IPPT session conducted during BMT, which means this IPPT score would be used in the evaluation on whether you are eligible for command school. All the previous IPPTs, I've only managed to pass, so I was hell bent on getting silver this time around. But alas, the heavens decided that that would not be my destiny for that day.

The first station was push-ups. Being technologically advanced (though I would argue technologically backwards), the SAF uses the ELISE machine to count the repetitions for static stations (push ups and sit ups). Unless your form is perfect, you would hear all sorts of nonsense coming from the machine such as "raise butt", "straighten back", "Go lower". It was infuriating but one had to live with it. The common tactic was that if your initial 20 repetitions registered multiple no counts, it would be better to retry the station rather than waste your energy continuing. Which is exactly what happened to me. I did my first 30, and it only registered 15, so I decided I would redo the station, no harm right?

Well sucks to be you Yin Jian because fate decided to give you the middle finger that day.

Here I am, my second time attempting the push up station. At this point, there were two female officers fiddling with the ELISE machine that was recording my attempt. I thought it was weird but they resassured me to carry on, and I did. The machine worked perfectly fine, registering all my repetitions, until around the 25th mark, when the machine just decided to give up on life and shut down.

What the fuck.

I looked at the two female officers fiddling with the ELISE machine and told them that it had turned off while I was doing my attempt.

"Oops! Sorry! You go try again"

WHAT THE FUCK???!!!! HELLO, I'VE ALREADY DONE CLOSE TO 60 PUSH UPS IN THE LAST 5 MINS. AM I SUPERMAN? DO YOU THINK I'M NOT TIRED?

At that point, hopes of getting silver was no longer in my mind, the idea of failing my last IPPT suddenly became very real.

I ended up doing the push-up station again and getting by with 33 repetitions.

Next was the sit-up station. Already pissed beyond belief, I just wanted to get this over and done with. I strapped myself down and started my sit-ups only to find out after giving it my all that the machine malfunctioned and recorded that I did 999 situps. What in the world? I went to the fitness instructor and told him of my predicament and all I got was to try again at another machine.

I must have been the fittest man alive that day because while everyone did one trial of IPPT, I think I did 2.

I ended up barely scrapping through the sit-up station.

Last but no least, we had our 2.4km run. At this point, I just wanted to pass IPPT and not think about it anymore. To hell with going to command school, 3 SIR just take me in please, anything to end my current misery. My score up till that point was teetering on failure (less that 60 points) and I had to run like my life depended on it just to even pass. And so I did. Except it turned out that I ran just enough to get 59 points.

I failed IPPT by one point...

You know what that means! Remedial training WOOHOO!

The next day, everybody got to book out on a Thursday night, while I had to stay back all the way till Friday evening to retake IPPT. By some stroke of luck, I managed to get silver.

Till this day, I don't know whether this incident was a blessing or a curse, because I believe retaking IPPT was the reason why I got into command school in the end.

The Night Blindness Incident
One thing that army really made me start to appreciate a lot more is just how brightly lit the city is. For those who have never been outfield before, you don't know how privileged we are to have such a good coverage of lighting in our streets and homes. I used to think that I was not afraid of the dark, because up till then, I have never experienced what was truly pitch-dark.

Once you are in the forest at night under dense vegetation without any artificial light or moonlight, you would understand how I feel. No one can see anything. You can't even see the person standing right in front of you, much less your own hands. There could be a pothole in front of you and you would be none the wiser, until you fall into it of course. When we were told to navigate around like this, I was scared shitless.

That's not even the worst thing.

During field camp, we had to sleep inside the shellscrape that we dug for a night, and I had the fright of my life when I woke up. Imagine having your eyes wide open but you see absolutely nothing. You can't even tell up from down. In fact, in that moment of confusion, I thought I had either gone blind or insane.

Nature is humbling in its own ways huh.

The Crying Incident
For those who know me, I would say I am not an emotionally expressive person. I tend to deal with my emotions internally, and I rarely display it for others to see. I believe the last time I cried before army was in primary school.

However, field camp did had me balling my eyes out.

I remember it was the fourth day of field camp, we hadn't seen civilisation for god knows how long, we hadn't eaten proper food for even longer. The PC decided to gather everyone together, and started reminding us on why NS exists, citing reasons about protecting our loved ones. The speech itself was not memorable nor was it emotionally-invoking, but for some reason, I just felt tears roll down my eyes.

Of course my dignity didn't allow me to cry in front of a whole bunch of men, so I tried to keep it in for a while. I soon realised that there were others around me crying too so I sort of just gave up and let loose.

Man, it was cathartic.

The Army has its ways of making you really miss your family and friends. Anyway, they commemorated that occasion by giving us fresh rations for the first time in so many days, along with 100 Plus! We were then each issued an envelope that was prepared by our family, which contained photos and a letter.

That was it for me. I cried so hard AHAHAH.

It was pitch dark at that point and they told us to write a letter back to our family. So there I was, a torchlight strapped on to my helmet, using a broken pen, with tears in my eyes and snot up my nose, I wrote the cringiest letter in my life.

Lesson learnt: Sometimes it's okay to cry my brothers, there is no shame in being vulnerable.

When Men Became Cowards Incident
What would BMT be without some sort of supernatural event! Unfortunately, this story isn't some encounter with some supernatural being, but rather how our whole platoon reacted to the haunting of another platoon.

On the last week of our BMT, the recruits of the section living above us started complaining about the banging and scratching of an unused locker in their bunk. The officer subsequently visited the bunk at night and confirmed their observations, there were indeed weird noises coming from the locker. However, there was nothing to be done about it. The officer left the recruits with his beret, because apparantly the Singapore Crest was blessed by all the different religions so it would serve as a deterrence to any supernatural events.

Upon getting wind of this, my bunk was scared shitless. What if our happy friend above decided to take a field trip one floor below into our bunk?

Hell fucking no. We were having none of it.

For the rest of the final week of BMT, we would carry our mattresses onto the floor and put it side by side with one another to sleep. Strength in numbers right! You would see people pulling out talismans and necklaces to ward off all kinds of evil, some even said prayers and did rituals.

Thinking back, this was so cowardly of us but I guess mass paranoia is not something to be underestimated!

The Final Stand-By-Bed Incident
Stand-By-Bed (SBB). The sadistic punishment that our superiors like to pull on us. For the uninitiated, there are three forms of cleaniness checks.

  • Stand-By-Area: This is the most common occurence. You are supposed to clean your bunk, the toilet and the common areas.
  • Stand-By-Bed: This is when your superiors are feeling cute. You are supposed to do everything in Stand-By-Area, but you have to open your locker and everything is held at higher standards.
  • Stand-By-Universe: They strip you of your dignity. You do everything in Stand-By-Bed and they will check literally every personal belonging from the inside out (even inside your underwear). Only done in extreme cases when there is theft or foul play involved.

On the last bookout before our 24km route march, we were subjected to the worst but funniest punishments I've ever had in BMT. We were given the period after lunch till last parade to prepare for SBB. Everyone was obviously excited because this was our final bookout, so we wanted to do a good job in cleaning to book out early. We spent 1 hour using the firehose to clean the corridor, the bunk and the toilet which turned out to be really effective. Honestly till today, I think that was the best cleaning I've ever done in my life.

Since we were section 4, we had to wait for the rest of the platoon to finish getting checked before it was our turn. While waiting in anticipation, you can hear the distant slamming of lockers and people shouting "Down! Down! Down!".

It was clearly not looking too hot.

When the first sergeant came to our section, took a peek inside the bunk, and straight up told us to hold the push up position. I think we ended up doing about 40 push ups and 20 crunches before he left.

Thinking it was over, we resumed chit-chatting and preparing for bookout.

Boy were we wrong.

The next sergeant came into our bunk and told us to knock it down again. Confused, we just did as we were told. They then began checking each person's bed one by one, going down the 4D number. At each bed, they would find the most ridiculous errors. Oh, your shoes are not aligned by 0.1cm? Push-up. There is one tiny speck of dust inside your locker? Push-up. I think you guys get the idea already.

This SBB was designed to fail. This ain't no stand by bed, this was a buttfuck session.

What ensued was absolute chaos, just constant screaming and shouting. There were 3 sergeants in the room, constantly running about, finding the smallest fault in our bunk. I remember at some point they pointed up towards the ceiling and asked the IC why there was dust up there. The IC simply had nothing to say other than that we didn't see it (Who the hell cleans the ceiling????). When they came to my bed, they asked why my pillow wasn't pulled. The reason why it wasn't pulled was because the previous sergeant threw it around, of course it wasn't going to be pulled after going through that treatment. But what can I say? I just nodded my head and faced the ground. In my haste to do my punishment, I kicked the door of my locker.

And the crowd went wild. More screaming and shouting.

I ended up doing each push-up while shouting "Sorry locker, I won't kick you again".

When they were finally done with everyone's bed, we all breathed a sigh of relief, thinking it was over. But nope. One of the sergeant said

"Section 4, I'm so disappointed in you today. Where are the usual high standards??!! We give you two hours to clean and this is the shit you give us? Nevermind, bring all your padlocks here and put it on the table."

Now what do we have here?

The sergeant proceeded to lock 16 padlocks together into what I can only call a lock-bomb.

"I want you all to find your own padlock and put it onto your locker. How long do y'all need? No one wants to answer? Okay, 30 seconds."

And so we had 30 seconds to defuse this lock-bomb. None of us had any idea what the key combination of each other's padlock were. To top it off, unlocking your own padlock usually took about 3 seconds because it involved turning the lock to three unique numbers, from clockwise to counter-clockwise to clockwise. How was it even possible to do this when all 16 padlocks are put together?

Like everything in SAF, this punishment was designed to fail.

What ensued was countless push-ups, crunches, bear-crawling and jumping jacks. We ended it off by being in the Senang-Diri position, except we were lying flat on the floor and kissing it for 10 minutes. Oh man, the things they make you do in the SAF are so wild.

And that was not even the end of it. We were then told to clean the bunk again for another inspection in 30 minutes. During that inspection, we were told to hold the push-up position at the corridor while they checked our room. I remember a hilarious exchange between the sergeant and my section-mate.

Sergeant: "Oi Diva, who said you can move!"

BMT in a nutshell
BMT was one hell of a ride, there were no highs and only lows. However, I guess the things we did were interesting? From the route marches, the songs we had to sing during our route march, the grenade throwing, the live firing, the Urban Ops, the field camp, and the mealtime regimes. Not forgetting to mention, the insanely late bookouts and early bookins we had because our company was located in School 4. Unlike School 1 to 3 that are located right beside the ferry terminal, School 4 is located at the very heart of Pulau Tekong, which means we have to take a vehicle for each book in and book out, and that translated to abysmal book-in/out timings.

There was also the final 24km route march, which was shit in and of itself, but I guess it made me appreciate Singapore a little more. The POP parade was kinda lit though, I should have thrown my jockey cap higher and further away!

With that said, what doesn't kill you makes you stronger and I would soon come to realise that BMT was a godsend compared to what awaited me in command school.

Beyond the best! Better than the rest! Bravo!

SCS

And we are on to the next phase of my NS journey, Specialist Cadet School (SCS)! Unfortunately, I wasn't good enough to go to Officer Cadet School (OCS), but oh well! No hard feelings!

SCS is a 6-months journey, where recruits will go first through foundation term for the first two months, learning basic infantry leader skills. They would then move on to the professional term, where they would be assigned to the various vocations such as Signals, Military Police, Navy, Medic, etc. Finally after everything, everyone will come back together for combined arms term, where we would practice for our Specialist Cadet Graduation Parade (SCGP).

As per above, I no longer remember things in chronological order, so you guys get short stories instead. Teehee!

The SAW Live Firing Incident
Every infantry section, comprising of 7 men, would carry weapon systems designed to tackle any obstacles that they encounter in battle. One of these weapon systems is the Section Automatic Weapon (SAW). It is basically build like a light machine-gun, so when you hold down the trigger, the gun goes brrrrrrrrrrrrrrrrr. The SAW is bulkier, longer and heavier than the SAR-21.

I do not enjoy handling the SAW because the charging handle always ends up poking my dick when I sling it. It's highly uncomfortable...

Anyway, each specialist cadet (SCT) had to clear SAW live firing as a veto to go to pro term. SAF treats live firing very seriously so movement of personnel during live firing is highly controlled. You want to go to the toilet? Ask your encik. You want to refill water? Ask your encik. You want to eat food? Ask your encik.

On this particular day, they didn't allow us to go to the toilet because it involved a very tedious process of checking the weapon for each indvidual. I, for one, am a huge fan of drinking water. On top of that, SAF makes us love drinking water by asking us to half-bottle drink up every hour. As such, my bladder was nearing its capacity but I was not scheduled to do live firing until after multiple more details. Attempts to beg my trainer to go to the toilet was rejected so the only option was to hold it in like a man.

For those who fired SAR-21, you would know how loud it is even with ear plugs on. You would also know the recoil when firing a bullet. Now you multiply that by about 3 times. You get the SAW. Not only is it fucking loud and definitely damaging to the eardrums, the recoil is insane because it is an automatic weapon compared to the SAR-21. In fact, I wonder how you even aim with the SAW, it definitely feels more like a spray-and-hope-it-hits-something kind of weapon to me.

Nothing could prepare me for the SAW live firing, not even the previous SAR-21 live firing that I had.

By the time it was my turn to do the live firing, my bladder was about to burst.

No man should experience what I experienced that day.

Let's just say, when the firing command was given, the SAW wasn't the only thing that fired that day.

The CBRE Incident
Chemical, Biological, Radiological and Explosive Disposal (CBRE), one of the coolest specialisations in the SAF! Though only a few selected individuals are chosen for this vocation, all SCTs would have to undergo basic CBRE training during foundation term. This involved wearing the Mission Oriented Protective Posture (MOPP) equipment. Just imagine yourself decked out in another layer of protective suit from head to toe, from a suit, to boots, gloves and gas masks.

Our training involved us being in the MOPP 4 outfit for 10 minutes, MOPP 4 being the highest level of protection against CBRE elements. Man was it hot, it was like being in a toaster, I felt like I was being cooked alive by the very thing I was wearing. When I finally got to take it off, though it was during the afternoon under the blazing hot sun, it legit felt like I just walked into an air-conditioned room.

Hats off to all the CBRE personnels, especially the guys, my god I don't know how you guys do it. Imagine your sperm getting cooked alive by your own body. Jesus.

The Infantry Pro Term Incident
Foundation term ends off with a bang, the Sergeant Rocky Challenge (SRC). SCTs will go around Pasir Laba Camp overnight completing challenges that would cover everything that we have learnt during the foundation term. It was supposed to be a milestone for everyone of us, signalling the end of days where we wear jocky cap and the days where we finally get to wear our berets. But alas, this was also the day where everyone would find out about their vocation posting.

Since we were part of the 3 SIR mono-intake, dreams postings such as Military Police, CBRE, the Navy were already out of the question. The only possible vocation that we could go to were the vocations that 3 SIR needed, namely Recon, Medic, Signals, Artillery and last but absolutely the least, Infantry.

From the get go, everyone's personal mission in foundation term was to escape infantry pro term. You might say recon is more tiring than infantry, but at least they get to do their own things during missions while wearing the jungle hat compared to the IQ-lowering helmet. Though medics have to carry the medic pack, their training was infintely more fun and relevant. Signals was of course the best posting since it involved a lot of studying and not a lot of outfields. Tekong Assault Mission Incident
If you ask me, infantry has the most boring and tiring job in a war.

You are too insignificant to make any realistic impact during a war because those are settled by the Air Force, Navy and long range weapon systems such as artillery.

Your only job in the war is to march in after the whole land has been razed by bombardments, fight a dirty fight from trench to trench, and occupy said land. And yet, the amount of load you have to carry is inhumane. I'm talking 20kg worth of load per person. It is truly a marvel how one can keep proning and recovering multiple times with that load. If I had to do this in a real war, I might just prone once and lie forever in the same spot.

For those who don't know, a typical infantry assault mission goes like this. During the daylight hours, you would stay put in a certain area, being in stand-two position (basically guns out and look out for enemies trespassing). After daylight hours, you would then start moving, before attacking the enemy at first light, when the sun just rises.

For those perceptive readers, you might wonder, wait Yin Jian, then when do you guys sleep?

That's the fun part. You don't.

From 9pm to 6am, you are walking towards your objective. From 6am to 8am, you are attacking your objective. From 8am onwards till 9pm, you are setting up defence around your objective, eating lunch and dinner. Theoretically, there's no time to sleep.

Realistically, many cadets end up falling asleep when we are doing stand-two. It's literally impossible not to. Imagine staring into blank space for 6 hours straight. Are we robots??? The trick is to get into the prone position and put your face at the scope of your rifle, and drift off to lalaland. Anyway back to the story! On this particular night walk, I was carrying the SAW. Ugh, I know! Since it was one of the first few attack missions, our encik would pause during certain periods of our night walk to allow us to rest. By convention, during these pauses, since we are walking in file formation (essentially two straight lines), we would have to go into high alert and point our weapons to our flanks, to cover every possible direction of attack. During this particular pause, our encik gave the specific instruction to rest, while also mentioning that we can sit down.

ITI

And we are into the final leg of my NS journey, this is the place I would call home for the next year and also the place where I met some of my best friends.

Infantry Training Institute (ITI) is found in Jurong Camp 2, which is coincidentally located right beside where I'm studying right now, Nanyang Technological University (NTU). And so, by any chance a random stranger on the Internet chances upon this while looking up ITI, I would like to share more about the life inside. Do note that during the year I ORDed, they were undergoing restructuring and hence things might not be the same as I remembered.

ITI has five main branches: Infantry Combat Training Centre 1 (ICTC 1), ICTC 2, Active Unit Training Centre (AUTC), Opposition Force (OPFOR) and finally Headquarters (HQ).

ICTC 1 and ICTC 2 serve similar functions. They are meant to train reservist infantry battallions. This includes infantry units, guard units and sometimes armour infantry units.

AUTC, as the name suggest, trains active units. These are combat units with NSFs who haven't ORDed. One example would be 3 SIR that I escaped from. These people would be in charge of your Army Training Evaluation Centre (ATEC).

OPFOR, are basically used during ATEC to simulate enemies. These are NSFs who are infantry trained, just with a hell lot less discipline. Since they are supposed to emulate our enemies, they tend to do weird shit on the battlefield.

HQ is at the heart of it all, where the Commander of the camp resides, and everybody reports to him.

Phew! That's the crash course for ITI. A lovely, tight-knit community with regulars who treat NSFs as equals. Truly a marvelous place to be at.

The TQP Incident
Trainer Qualification Programme (TQP), something that all commanders posted to ITI would have to go through. Held 4 times a year to sync with the arrivals of new NSF sergeants, it is basically a 2 weeks course to induct you properly into ITI.

First, you would attend a course at SAFTI MI where they would teach you on how to be an effective trainer. This course lasts a week. You would then come back to ITI and finish the rest of the necessary lessons such as safety, recapping on your infantry skills, so on and so forth.

A very chill two weeks if I do say so myself. You don't have a routine order to follow at all, just wake up at anytime, decide whether you even want to eat breakfast, just make sure you make it to the lessons on time. Everyday should end at 5pm and they would not touch you. Literally the DREAM life! Oh, did I mention, there's free wifi throughout the camp. YES! FREE WIFI! You can game, you can gym, you can practically do anything. Oh my god, thinking back, this was really the god posting.

But be prepared my brothers. At the end of TQP, there is what they call the Commander's PT. This man is in his late fourties, but he sure as hell is 10 times fitter than you will ever be. All ITI commanders I have met are 100 pointers in IPPT. Jesus.

This PT is designed to destroy you. From push-ups, to sit-ups, to leopard and bear crawls, to lunges, to sprints, to jogging, to staircase climbing, to pull-ups, to SOC, to tyre flipping. The list is non-exhaustive. Whatever that mad man can think of, he will ask you to do.

BUT, he will do it with you.

That's the sign of a true leader.

I, for one, having enjoyed the cozy life, had gotten extremely unfit. I almost vomitted twice during the PT. How shameful.

Once the perpetual torture that is the Commander PT ends, every new individual would be welcomed to the induction ceremony. There, each high ranking individual of the five respective branches of ITI would say well wishes to everyone, before adding an ingredient into the frankenstein concoction.
